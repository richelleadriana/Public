### Post 1 - 3d

Everyone at Cannes wanted to talk about AI search, but very few are talking about what comes after it.

Throughout the week, there were dozens of conversations about how AI is eating the SERP, how brands need to show up in AI answers, and how citations are the new backlinks.

That fight is already underway, and most marketers know they need to do something about it.

There's a second wave coming, and it's going to hit a lot harder than the first one.

Personalization.

Not the watered down kind where you get a generic location inferred response.

Real personalization, where the AI answering a query already knows who's asking and shapes the response around that person specifically.

Think about what that breaks when it comes to the way companies are setting up their LLM marketing strategies.

Marketing has spent two decades building campaigns around personas. A fictional 35-year-old VP of Marketing at a mid-market SaaS company who cares about ROI and has three kids.

That persona was always a compromise, a way to mass-produce relevance for thousands of different real humans who didn't actually fit the mold. AI search no longer needs to compromise because it can talk to the actual person in front of it.

That means the generic keyword-driven content built for "best XYZ software" stops working the way it used to, because the AI isn't matching keywords anymore, it's matching the user.

A SaaS founder asking about software gets a completely different answer than an enterprise IT buyer asking the exact same question.

This is going to be brutal for marketers who built their entire stack around average.

There is no average customer anymore in an AI-mediated world.

There's just the customer, the actual one, sitting on the other side of the prompt.

This will be very good for users. AI that actually understands an individual buyer's needs and skips the generic response provides a better experience.

Companies that keep building for a generic best customer are about to find out how fast generic stops converting.

### Post 2 - 1d

Stop calling your blog posts SEO. They're not.

So many of my potential consulting leads have this question: what should they do with their SEO blog now that they suspect it doesn't work?

A company publishes two posts a week, targets high-volume keywords, and follows every rule the agency gave them.

Yet, traffic is flat and they want to know what's broken.

This isn't SEO. At best, it's content marketing, and since nobody wrote it for an actual audience, not even that. It's just content, sold to you as SEO, by people who needed something to bill for.

Real SEO has one job: make your product findable at the exact moment someone's ready to use it. That's it.

Content marketing and SEO are not the same function. One is measured in subscribers and brand recall. The other is measured in signups, trials, and pipeline. Stop grading them on the same curve.

Find the moment of intent and put the product there. Everything else is just writing.

### Post 3 - 17h

How long does it take to impact AI overviews?

This was my favorite question from my session on AI visibility at the Cannes Lions International Festival of Creativity Semrush Villa with Leigh McKenzie and Eli Goodman.

This question showed both the intense curiosity around this channel and the old fashioned SEO thinking of seeking immediate results.

The answer of course is dependent on many things.

For a large brand that missed out on a few low hanging opportunities, it could be hours to days before results show up.

For a startup it could be years.

While there are some quick hacks in this channel, as time goes on they will be fewer and further between.

As with all things marketing, time is a moat.
